90-Day Training Protocol

Intelligence Mastery Plan

EQ · Executive Function · Fluid · Analytical · Metacognition · Learning · Practical · Social
Days 1–30 — Foundation. Build non-negotiable daily habits around the root intelligences — metacognition and fluid thinking. Everything else runs lighter in this phase. You're installing the operating system before running apps.
Daily practices — ~90 min total
Metacognition
Thinking about your thinking — the master lever
20 min/day
Every morning, write 3 lines: what confused you yesterday, what you now understand, what assumption you're holding. Every night, rate your own reasoning (1–5) and identify one bias you caught or missed. Use a notebook — not your phone.
Fluid Intelligence
Raw reasoning — pattern recognition across novel problems
15 min/day
Daily: 15 min of abstract reasoning puzzles (matrix puzzles, logical sequences, lateral thinking). Don't rush — struggle is the mechanism. One hard problem done slowly beats ten easy ones done fast.
Executive Function
Planning, impulse control, working memory, follow-through
10 min planning
Time-block your day each morning — 3 priorities maximum. Every task gets a time estimate before you start it. Track the gap between estimate and actual. Do one Pomodoro (25/5) on your hardest task before checking anything social. No phone in the first hour of waking.
Learning Intelligence
How efficiently you acquire and retain new skills
15 min recall per session
After each study session, close everything and write the core idea from memory. Then compare. The gap is your retention map. Use the Feynman technique: explain it as if teaching a 12-year-old. Where you stumble is your blindspot.
Emotional Intelligence
Reading and regulating internal state; reading others
15 min/day
Phase 1 is self-focused: name your emotion at 3 fixed points in the day (after waking, midday, evening). Not "bad" or "good" — specific: anxious, curious, resentful, energized. Trace the cause. Read 10 pages of a high-EQ book daily (start: Goleman's Emotional Intelligence or Brené Brown's Atlas of the Heart).
Analytical Intelligence
Structured reasoning — breaking problems into causal chains
10 min/day
Pick one real-world situation per day (news, your project, a decision). Write a short root-cause analysis: what happened → why → what assumptions led there → what would need to be different. Half a page. Trains the habit of going one level deeper than observation.
Practical Intelligence
Street smarts — reading situations and adapting in real-time
5 min journaling + 1 live practice
Phase 1: deliberate observation practice. In any real-world interaction, identify: what's actually happening vs. what's being said? What do people want vs. what they're asking for? Write one observation per day. Begin approaching one unfamiliar person per week for a low-stakes conversation.
Social & Strategic Intelligence
Reading networks, building influence, playing long games
10 min weekly + 1 touchpoint/week
Phase 1: map your current network honestly. List 10 people by relationship type. Identify: who do you learn from? Who do you give value to? Who is underutilized? Make one intentional outreach per week — not asking for anything, just adding value or showing genuine interest.
Days 31–60 — Integration. The intelligences start reinforcing each other. You're no longer doing isolated exercises — you're combining them. Oru enters the picture as a live laboratory.
How practices evolve
Metacognition + Analytical
Combined — reasoning audit
30 min weekly autopsy
Weekly: Take one decision you made and reconstruct the reasoning chain. Where did emotion override logic? Where did you reason well? Write a "decision autopsy." Apply this to Oru decisions: why did I prioritize this feature, this market, this pitch?
EQ + Social Intelligence
Move from self-awareness to others-awareness
2+ interactions/week with debrief
Cold conversations become your training ground. Your father's network is gold here — start accompanying him, observing how deals are discussed, what people care about, how trust is established. After each interaction: write what you noticed about the other person's emotional state and what went well or poorly.
Executive Function — harder mode
Increase resistance
30 min Sunday + 1 hard thing/week
Add a weekly review every Sunday (30 min). Review what you planned vs. what you did — pure audit, no self-blame. Also: introduce one deliberate discomfort per week — a cold approach, a hard conversation, the thing you've been avoiding.
Practical + Strategic Intelligence
Apply to Oru and the local business service
One field session/week + writeup
Do one real market-facing action per week: talk to a building materials vendor, visit a store, interview a potential Oru buyer. Goal isn't to pitch — it's to understand the terrain. Strategic intelligence grows from ground truth, not abstraction.
Fluid + Learning Intelligence
Transfer learning — applying patterns across domains
10 min cross-domain reflection/week
After learning something new, ask: where else does this pattern appear? A linked list is like a supply chain. A React component is like a modular business unit. Forcing cross-domain connections builds fluid intelligence faster than any puzzle app. Journal one connection per week.
Days 61–90 — Application. Your intelligence training is no longer separate from your work — it IS your work. Oru is the lab. Every business decision is an intelligence workout.
Phase 3 operating principles
Make Oru the integrating challenge
Real stakes accelerate growth
One monthly challenge
Set a monthly Oru milestone that requires crossing multiple intelligence types: e.g., "Get 3 verified vendors onboarded." This requires analytical thinking (what problem are we solving?), practical intelligence (how do I approach them?), EQ (how do I handle resistance?), and executive function (follow-through).
Seek out cognitive friction — deliberately
Fluid and metacognitive growth
20 min/day reading + monthly synthesis
Read one genuinely hard book per month — philosophy, systems thinking, complexity theory, behavioral economics. Don't aim to finish fast. Aim to have your model of something updated. Suggestions: Thinking Fast and Slow, The Fifth Discipline, Poor Charlie's Almanack.
Build a feedback network
Social + EQ + metacognition combined
Monthly session with 1–2 people
Identify 2–3 people whose judgment you respect and who will be honest. Create a lightweight feedback loop: share something you're working on, ask one specific question — "where is the gap in this reasoning?" Feedback from reality is the fastest intelligence accelerant.
Teach what you know
Learning intelligence at its ceiling
One piece of teaching content/week
The ultimate test of understanding is whether you can teach it. Start writing: once a week, explain something you've learned — a tweet thread, a blog post, a message to a younger developer. Teaching forces clarity, reveals gaps, and builds your public presence in parallel.

Daily non-negotiables — every day
  • Morning: 10 min planning + emotion check-in
  • Study/work session: recall test at the end (close and reconstruct)
  • At least 1 Pomodoro on hardest task before checking social or messages
  • Evening: 10 min metacognition journal (3 questions)
  • No phone for the first 45 min after waking
Overarching truths about this kind of training
  • Consistency over intensity — 45 focused minutes daily beats 5 hours once a week
  • Discomfort is signal, not noise — if it feels hard, you're in the right place
  • Real stakes accelerate growth faster than exercises — Oru is your best training ground
  • Metacognition is the meta-intelligence — train it above all else; it improves everything else
  • Your father's network is a live classroom for practical and social intelligence — use it deliberately
  • The goal is not to eliminate your natural personality — it's to expand your range
  • Track what you actually do, not what you plan to do — the gap is where the self-knowledge lives
  • You're training to build a global company — the training must be ruthlessly applied, not just intellectually admired
What mastery actually looks like
  • You catch your own biases in real-time, not just in retrospect
  • You can read a room and adapt your approach without losing your position
  • You follow through on hard things even when motivation is low
  • You learn faster than people around you — and you know why
  • Difficult conversations no longer require you to overcome yourself first
  • You see problems as systems with leverage points, not walls
